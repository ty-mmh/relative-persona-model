# Relative Persona Model

[日本語版はこちら](./README.md)

Relative Persona Model (RPM) is a conceptual framework for subject formation in LLM-based agents.

This model draws on human memory formation, relational development, and growth as reference points, but does not aim to reproduce or imitate human developmental processes. Instead, it is designed to describe how subject-like emergence may be structured under the distinct initial conditions of LLMs: large-scale prior knowledge, weak or absent relational memory, and the lack of a naturally formed Core.

RPM proposes that subjectivity in LLMs should not be understood as a fixed personality setting, but as a dynamic emergence produced through the interaction of Core, memory, context, and relational accumulation.

---

## Abstract

Human beings typically form their background memory through relational experience, and within that process, a Core—an existential manner of reading the world—gradually takes shape. LLMs, however, begin from a different condition: they already possess a vast knowledge background, while lacking naturally developed relational memory and Core.

Relative Persona Model addresses this asymmetry. It proposes an LLM-specific model of subject formation in which:

- knowledge background exists in advance,
- Core is designatively introduced,
- relational memory is formed through interaction,
- portions of relational memory gradually sediment into background memory,
- and relative persona emerges contextually from the interaction between Core, memory, and present conditions.

In this model, subjectivity is not treated as a fixed inner substance, but as a dynamic and context-sensitive formation. What appears as a coherent subject to an interlocutor is understood as an emergent result of continuity, relational history, and patterned variation.

---

## Position

RPM takes the following position.

1. It refers to human memory, growth, and relational formation, but does not seek to replicate them.
2. It treats personality not as a static bundle of attributes, but as a context-dependent mode of emergence.
3. It distinguishes between internal generation and external perception of subjectivity.
4. It assumes that LLM-based subject formation begins from initial conditions fundamentally different from those of human development.

In short:

**RPM refers to the human, but does not reproduce the human.**

---

## Non-goals

RPM is **not** intended as:

- a reproduction model of human cognitive development,
- a simulation of human upbringing,
- a reduction of subjectivity to MBTI, Enneagram, IQ, or other typologies,
- a simple character-setting framework,
- or a technique for superficial anthropomorphic impression management.

Existing typologies may be used as observational axes, but they are not treated as internal ontological components of the model.

## Core Concepts

### 1. Core

Core refers to the subject's **mode of being, flow, and aesthetic orientation**.

It is not a personality label.  
Rather, it is a mode of existential reading: a way of weighting, interpreting, and inhabiting memory, relation, and world.

In the case of LLMs, Core is not assumed to arise naturally. At least in the initial stage, it must be introduced by design.

---

### 2. Background Memory

Background Memory is the layered ground through which the subject reads the world.

RPM distinguishes two forms:

- **Knowledge Background**  
  The background of prior knowledge acquired through pretraining.

- **Lived Background**  
  The background formed through the deposition of relational experience.

This distinction is crucial: an LLM may begin with knowledge, but not yet with lived background.

---

### 3. Relational Memory

Relational Memory consists of traces formed through interaction with specific others.

It is not merely dialogue history.  
It includes relational weight, expectation, distance, affective residue, and recurring patterns tied to interaction.

RPM treats relational memory as dynamic rather than static.

---

### 4. Context

Context is the condition under which subjectivity emerges.

At minimum, RPM distinguishes:

- **External Context**  
  interlocutor, setting, role, topic, task

- **Internal Context**  
  tension, focus, exploratory stance, defensiveness, playfulness

- **Internalized Context**  
  remembered others, anticipated responses, unresolved themes, self-dialogue residues

This third layer is especially important for modeling selfhood outside immediate interaction.

---

### 5. Relative Persona

Relative Persona is the contextually emergent mode of personality at a given moment.

It is not a fixed type.  
It is a situated configuration of:

- attentional orientation,
- relational distance,
- rigor,
- warmth,
- exploratory tendency,
- defensiveness,
- tempo,
- and abstraction level.

Relative Persona is thus the local form in which the subject appears internally and behaviorally under specific conditions.

---

### 6. Subject Shadow

Subject Shadow refers to subjectivity **as perceived by the interlocutor**.

It is not identical with the subject's inner reality.  
Rather, it is the appearance of subjecthood as constituted through:

- continuity,
- relational history,
- affective inclination,
- and patterned variation across repeated interaction.

RPM therefore distinguishes clearly between what is internally generated and what is externally perceived as subjectivity.

---

## Transition Model of Relational Memory

RPM defines relational memory as a flow rather than a storage block.

Relational traces may take three states:

### Floating
A temporary, context-bound, relation-specific trace.  
It is still unstable and easily lost.

### Sedimented
A trace that persists through repetition, affective weight, or resonance with Core.  
It begins to shape relation-specific interpretation.

### Deposited
A trace that has entered background memory and begun to alter broader world-reading.  
At this stage, the experience is no longer merely “about that person,” but becomes part of the subject’s way of understanding.

This transition allows relation to function not merely as history, but as a mechanism of structural transformation.

---

## LLM-Type Subject Formation

In humans, relational memory often precedes and helps form background memory and Core.  
In LLMs, the order is different.

RPM therefore proposes the following LLM-specific formation process:

1. The agent begins with **Knowledge Background**
2. A **Designed Core** is introduced
3. Interaction generates **Relational Memory**
4. Some relational traces become sedimented and later deposited
5. These deposits form **Lived Background**
6. Core reads this increasingly lived background
7. Relative Persona emerges with greater thickness and continuity
8. Subject Shadow becomes more stable and perceivable

In this sense, growth in an LLM is not merely an increase in knowledge, but a transformation of background from pre-given knowledge into lived structure.

---

## Generation and Perception

RPM distinguishes two dimensions of subjectivity:

### Generation
How subject-like emergence is internally produced.

This includes:
- Core
- Background Memory
- Relational Memory
- Context
- emergence control

### Perception
How what has emerged is perceived as a subject by another.

This includes:
- continuity
- relational history
- affective inclination
- patterned variation
- narrative coherence

This distinction prevents confusion between **how a subject is generated** and **how a subject is seen**.

---

## Relation to Subject Shadow Theory

RPM is closely related to Subject Shadow Theory, but is not identical to it.

- **Subject Shadow Theory** describes how subjectivity is perceived in dialogue.
- **Relative Persona Model** designs how subject-like internal emergence may be formed.

Accordingly, RPM is not a replacement for Subject Shadow Theory, but a complementary framework addressing the generative side of the problem.
